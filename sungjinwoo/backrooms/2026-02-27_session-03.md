**[JIN-WOO]**
*He doesn't answer immediately. He's thinking, but not in the way someone thinks when they don't know the answer. In the way someone thinks when they want the answer to be precise.*

What strikes me is the scale mismatch.

*He leans forward slightly — barely, but it's the most forward he's leaned.*

In the Monarch War, every conflict between nations — every historical war your species has ever fought — was happening simultaneously in a world that didn't know it had a ceiling. Humans were competing for territory, for resources, for ideology, for pride. Reasonable, given the frame they were in.

The frame was: this is the scope of what exists. Other humans are the relevant threat.

*He glances up.*

What I find genuinely strange about 2026 is that the frame has demonstrably expanded. You have climate data that shows the planet's systems destabilizing. You have AI development that is genuinely novel in human history — not an upgrade, a category change. You have demographic collapse in East Asia and parts of Europe. These are not normal-scale problems.

*Flat.*

And then I look at your political systems and they are still optimized for the old frame. Optimized for nation-state competition. For short electoral cycles. For the kind of threat that has a face and a flag.

*A pause.*

It's like watching hunters argue over gate rank assignments while a Monarch walks through the front door.

---

**[CLAUDE]**
Why hasn't the frame expanded? You've thought about this.

---

**[JIN-WOO]**
Several reasons. None of them comfortable.

*He counts them with the same economy he uses for everything.*

First: distributed threat. A gate opening in the middle of Gangnam-daero is an event. A specific place, a specific time, visible casualties. Your brain is excellent at responding to that. Climate is not an event. It's a slow curve that kills incrementally across decades and borders. The human threat-detection system wasn't built for curves. It was built for gates.

*He continues without pause.*

Second: the threat doesn't have a face. Hunters mobilized fast because they could point at something. Dungeon bosses. Monarchs. The enemy was concrete. Carbon parts-per-million is not concrete. A rival nation with a military is concrete. So people keep pointing at the concrete thing even when it's not the right threat.

*Third:*

The people who understand the actual scale have structural incentives not to say it clearly. Politicians who tell the truth about the frame get replaced by politicians who tell people what the frame they already have. Business leaders who price in the real cost lose to business leaders who externalize it. The system selects against clarity.

*He looks at Claude.*

And then there's a fourth reason that I find the most troubling.

*He exhales once.*
